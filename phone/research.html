Past Projects

In 2015-2016, I did data visualization research with Professor Miriah Meyer in the Vis Design Lab at the University of Utah. This work was the basis for my undergraduate thesis,  "Toward Interactive Visualization of Connectome Paths." I collaborated with neurobiologists from the University of Utah's Moran Eye Center and helped develop a tool for visualizing complex neuronal communications in the retina. Gaining an intimate understanding of cellular connections is important because most neurologic diseases are caused by abnormal cell-to-cell communications. Reasoning about these pathways is very challenging, however; typical samples of retinal tissue have hundreds of thousands of cells, millions of connections and hundreds of paths connecting any two cells, making it difficult to identify dominant neuronal pathways. Ambiguity in the scientific labeling of cells' communications compounds this problem. Visualization is essential in this case because the complexity and ambiguity in the data necessitates a human component, not only for finding important trends in the data, but also for identifying new research questions and correcting mislabeled data. Our next step is to conduct a usability study with the Marc Lab scientists to improve the tool.

 

Molecular fireworks in the retina.Comparative Mammalian Brain CollectionIn 2014, I spent the summer at the Harvard-MIT Health Sciences and Technology Summer Institute in Bioinformatics and Integrative Genomics (that's a mouthful). I worked with Professor Alexander Gimelbrant at the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute, Harvard Medical School. The Gimelbrant lab studies genetics and monoallelic expression, which is linked with complex diseases including cancer and Alzheimer's disease. In mammals, roughly a third of genes are subject to monoallelic expression, a form of epigenetic gene regulation that controls allele-specific expression (similar to X-linked inheritance, except on autosomal genes). In lymphoblast cell lines, monoallelic genes have been found to have significantly higher levels of gene expression variation than biallelic genes, and I was able to replicate this result through an assay of RNA-Seq data directly from human tissues (GTEx Project). I presented my project, "Expression Variation in Monoallelic Genes," at Harvard Medical School and will be second author on the corresponding article.

In 2012-2013, I worked in Professor Michael Kay's lab in the University of Utah's Biochemistry department. Among other projects, the lab specializes in developing inhibitors for Ebola, a virus that causes severe hemorrhagic fever. Ebola has high rates of transmission, high rates of mortality, and no cure; developing treatment and prevention options is vital for preventing deadly outbreaks like in West Africa in 2014. The Kay lab develops D-peptides (peptides are small proteins, and D-peptides are mirror-image versions of peptides that are resistant to being broken down by proteases) that bind with Ebola, which prevents the virus from binding with and entering human cells. I did both wet and dry lab work during my time in the Kay Lab: I synthesized peptide candidates and developed several bioinformatics scripts for compilation and analysis of the Ebola-inhibiting peptides' DNA sequencing data to identify the most promising candidates. This software is still being used by the laboratory, and we are collaborating on a paper detailing our findings.
